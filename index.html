01 / Overview

Abstract

A research examination of how dance ability, attitudes toward dance, and church involvement compare across Seventh-day Adventist students and faculty.

Dance occupies a culturally contested place in Seventh-day Adventist tradition, with historic guidance cautioning against social dance even as younger members increasingly view structured forms — line dance, choreographed worship — as compatible with practice. Whether differences in attitudes toward dance translate into measurable differences in dance ability, and whether either tracks with how engaged a member is with their church, has rarely been examined empirically.

This study compared 41 Adventist participants (17 faculty/professors; 24 students) on three measures: a self-reported church-involvement index (SDA index), a self-reported attitudinal Dancing index, and an observed Dance performance score on a structured line-dance task. Group comparisons were conducted in Jamovi using nonparametric and parametric tests appropriate to each variable's distribution.

Self-reported church involvement did not differ between students and faculty, suggesting comparable Adventist cultural exposure across the two groups. Despite this similarity, students reported substantially more permissive attitudes toward dance than faculty and scored higher on the observed dance task. These findings suggest that dance ability and attitudes may be shaped by generational, social, or educational factors operating alongside, rather than mirroring, religious involvement.

02 / Research Question

What we asked

Does church involvement differ between groups?

Are students and faculty/professors equally engaged with the church, as measured by self-reported SDA index?

Do attitudes toward dance differ?

Do students and faculty/professors hold different views on the acceptability and value of dance, as captured by the Dancing index?

Does dance performance differ?

Do students and faculty/professors differ in their observed ability to perform a structured line dance, scored on a standardized rubric?

03 / Background

Why this question matters

Dance has historically been a contested practice within Seventh-day Adventism. Denominational guidance has cautioned against social dance as inconsistent with reverent embodiment, while younger members increasingly view structured movement — line dance, choreographed worship — as benign or even devotional. This generational shift in attitude has been described informally but rarely studied empirically.

At the same time, embodied behaviors are recognized in the broader literature as meaningful indicators of group identity, social comfort, and shared practice. Whether attitudes toward a culturally disputed activity like dance also correspond to measurable differences in actual dance ability — and whether either dimension tracks with religious engagement — is largely unknown in this community.

This study addresses that gap by comparing self-reported attitudes, observed dance ability, and self-reported church involvement across two Adventist groups: students (predominantly aged 18–24) and faculty/professors (predominantly aged 35+) at a single Adventist institution.

04 / Methods

How we studied it

All analyses were conducted in Jamovi 2.7 using GAMLj (general linear model) and built-in nonparametric tests.

Participants

N = 41 self-identified Seventh-day Adventists at a single SDA institution. Convenience sample comprising 17 faculty/professors and 24 students. Age distribution ranged from 18–24 (n = 24) through 65+ (n = 3).

Measures

Three primary outcomes were collected: a self-reported SDA index capturing church involvement; a self-reported attitudinal Dancing index; and an observed Dance performance score assigned by trained raters using a structured rubric applied to a single line-dance performance.

Procedure

Participants completed the survey, watched a short video demonstration of the line-dance routine, and performed the routine once. Performances were scored by trained raters using a standardized rubric.

Analysis

Group comparisons used independent-samples t-tests (general linear models) when assumptions were met, and the Kruskal–Wallis test for the SDA index because Levene's test indicated unequal variances. Pearson correlations examined pairwise associations among the three measures. α = .05, two-tailed.

05 / Key Findings

What we found

Three findings worth holding together: a meaningful similarity, a strong difference, and a modest supporting one.

Informative null · Comparable cultural exposure

Church involvement was statistically indistinguishable between groups.

Self-reported SDA index scores were nearly identical for faculty/professors (M = 11.7) and students (M = 12.0), suggesting that participants in both groups had comparable exposure to Adventist cultural and church involvement. This shared baseline lets us isolate the more meaningful differences that follow.

χ²(1) = 0.13, p = .72
Primary finding · Strongly significant

Students reported substantially more permissive attitudes toward dance.

Despite the similar cultural exposure indicated by the SDA index, students scored more than twice as high on the Dancing index (M = 6.50) as faculty/professors (M = 2.88), a large and highly significant effect. This suggests that attitudes toward dance shift markedly across groups even when broader religious involvement does not.

F(1, 39) ≈ 14, p < .001, η²p ≈ .25
Supporting finding · Significant at threshold

Students also scored higher on observed dance performance.

Students outperformed faculty/professors on the rubric-scored line-dance task (M = 13.75 vs. M = 10.94). The result reached the conventional significance threshold, with a moderate standardized effect, though the lower bound of the confidence interval brushed zero — a result we interpret as suggestive and worth replicating.

F(1, 39) = 4.10, p = .050, η²p = .10

“Although SDA index did not significantly differ between students and faculty, dance performance did. Similar exposure to Adventist culture does not necessarily predict similar dance performance — generational and social factors may play a larger role in shaping comfort, confidence, and embodied movement.”

08 / Discussion

Interpreting the results

Three findings emerged from this study, and each contributes to the same broader question: how do religious culture, generational identity, and embodied behavior interact within an Adventist community?

First, students and faculty/professors reported essentially identical levels of church involvement. We treat this informative null as a strength of the design rather than an absence of finding: it suggests that participants in both groups had comparable exposure to Adventist cultural and religious life, which provides a useful baseline against which more specific differences can be evaluated.

Second, despite that shared cultural baseline, the two groups differed sharply in their attitudes toward dance. Students were substantially more permissive on the Dancing index, with a large effect that is unlikely to be a sampling artifact even at this sample size. This pattern raises important questions about how attitudes toward culturally contested practices shift across generations within a single faith community.

Third, students also performed somewhat better on the observed line-dance task. Although this result reached significance at the conventional threshold, the confidence interval included values close to zero and we interpret the finding as suggestive rather than conclusive — a signal that warrants confirmation in a larger sample.

Read together, these findings may indicate that similar exposure to Adventist culture does not necessarily predict similar dance attitudes or dance performance. Generational, social, educational, or comfort-related factors appear to operate alongside religious involvement and may shape embodied behaviors such as dance in ways that simple measures of church involvement cannot capture. We do not claim a causal relationship; instead we offer this pattern as a contribution to the broader conversation about how religious identity and embodied practice interact, and as a starting point for more rigorous follow-up.

09 / Limitations

Honest scope of this study

This is an exploratory student research project; the limitations below should guide how the findings are read.

  • Small convenience sample. N = 41 from a single Adventist institution limits both statistical power and generalization. Only moderate-to-large effects could be reliably detected.
  • Unequal group sizes and limited older-generation representation. 17 faculty vs. 24 students; the 25–34 age band contained only one participant, leaving a gap between the dominant student (18–24) and faculty (35+) age clusters.
  • Self-report bias. Both the SDA index and Dancing index rely on self-report, and faculty in particular may underreport positive attitudes toward a culturally disputed practice.
  • Single dance task. Dance performance was based on one observed line dance. Inter-rater agreement for the rubric should be reported transparently to support the borderline performance result.
  • Cross-sectional design. No causal claims are warranted; we cannot speak to how attitudes, performance, or involvement develop over time.
  • Confounded categories. Standing (faculty vs. student) is closely correlated with age and life stage, so the observed group differences should be understood as a composite of those factors rather than the effect of standing alone.
10 / Future Research

Where this leads

Multi-site replication

Recruit Adventist participants from multiple campuses and congregations to test whether the student–faculty divergence in dance attitudes generalizes beyond a single institution.

Validated measures

Develop or adopt validated instruments for both attitudes toward dance and observed dance ability, including inter-rater reliability statistics on the rubric.

Disentangle age from role

Future studies should oversample the 25–34 and 50–64 bands to separate generational effects from the institutional roles of "student" and "faculty," which are otherwise confounded.

Qualitative interviews

Pair quantitative measures with semi-structured interviews to understand how participants reason about dance in relation to faith, generation, and community belonging.
